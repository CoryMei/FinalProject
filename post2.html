We have less control over
who we are and
what we really believe.

Cory Mei


"Exploring how a data company named Cambridge Analytica came to symbolise the dark side of social media in the wake of the 2016 U.S. presidential election, as uncovered by journalist Carole Cadwalladr." — Netflix: The Great Hack

With the development of modern science and technology and the popularization of digital equipment, the growth of data is explosive in today's society. Massive data is produced regularly through a variety of data sources. A Netflix documentary, "The Great Hack," focuses on the privacy issues in the era of big data from multiple perspectives. It addresses a reality of today: the practice of collecting personal data and using it to shape people's decisions has spread around the world and permeated every aspect of  modern Internet life. Everything we do on the Internet -- every interaction, every purchase, every web search, every location, every thumb up -- is carefully collected in real time into a trillion-dollar-a-year industry . Companies know our personality preferences and craft content that belongs only to us. They know what we like, what we fear, what we are interested in, and where to cross the line. They took your data, Then they took control. It presents an information security gap to the audience. And this huge gap is the nonstandard network market environment.

In the United States, every Internet user has 5000 data points: from social contact, family and work, to hobbies, daily life and entertainment. Based on the frequency that people search for an event, a certain product, or even a certain keyword, big data will recommend enough content and information to stimulate personal interest and then generate attention stickiness to users, and this is actually the way Facebook is working on. This is the era of big data at its best and worst. Data indeed facilitates our life to a certain extent, but some organizations or companies cross the line in gathering and using customer data. People lack knowledge of what, when, and how their private data are being used. The targeting tool also becomes a weapon for some organizations and companies to control, influence and change the public. Cambridge Analytica, a big data marketing agency, used Facebook's strong user base and rich social information to analyze and select “The Persuadables” among the Internet users and put them with well-matched political advertisements and information, which intervenes in The 2016 US presidential election and The UK's Brexit. The data analytics firm that worked with Donald Trump’s election team and the winning Brexit campaign harvested more than 50 millions of individual profiles, in one of the tech giant’s biggest ever data breaches”, and without authorization, used those data to build a powerful algorithm to analyze, predict and influence choices at the ballot box by determining personality traits linked to voting behavior. These platforms which were created to connect people, have now been “weaponized”. They targeted those whose minds they thought they could change until they saw the world the way they wanted them to. Once people agreed to plug their identities and lives into social networks, they left themselves open to being analyzed, manipulated and propagandized. And there is evidence that Facebook knew this from the beginning, but it did not take any effective measures to prevent the leakage of user information. This is why the American people are so angry about Facebook.
